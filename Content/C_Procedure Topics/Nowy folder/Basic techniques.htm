Basic techniques

Flooding

A technique called flooding involves making an outline with line icing and then filling the outlined area with flooding consistency icing. Outlining your design before flooding creates a 'dam' and prevents the thinner icing from spreading out. When the flooded icing sets hard, your cookie is ready for the next level of decorating - you can paint or write on the top of it, pipe your desired design or add other elements like flowers or transfers.

Stenciling

Cookie stencils are the easiest way to create professional-looking, uniform designs on your cookies while giving them dimension and texture (the stencil pattern stands out from the rest of the cookie). To use a cookie stencil, place it on top of the cookies flooded with base icing, apply thick icing with a spatula or a knife and then scrape it over top. When you remove your stencil, you have a ready design. You can repeat that design exactly the same way on as many cookies as you want.

RI transfers

RI transfers are decorations piped directly on a piece of wax/parchment paper or an acetate sheet, and left to dry. Once they dry, you may remove (transfer) them from the backing material and "glue" them onto the cookie with a small dot of icing or store in an airtight container at room temperature for future use. You can use any consistency royal icing, although it's better to keep it on the thick side (at least 20 second count). Avoid using icing that is too runny since it spreads fast and is difficult to keep a desired shape.

Note:

  • This method is very handy especially for newer decorators, making them able to create design elements in a low-pressure way. You can practice and make as many of a design elements as you want and add to your cookie later.
  • Transfers are a good option if you worry about the color bleed. It can be a safe way to add a design element without the bleed.
  • They're an excellent choice when you want to add metallic-painted elements (letters or numbers for example) to your cookie. It’s easier to create a transfer, conveniently paint it and then add to your cookie versus piping the design on and decorating it when it’s on the cookie.

Wet-on-wet technique

Normally, when decorating with RI, you create a "base layer" by flooding the cookie and, after it dries hard, you add details on top (by piping, painting etc.). However, in case of wet-on-wet technique, you decorate the cookie with liquid (wet) icing immediately after flooding your base layer, when it’s still "wet" (hence the name). The details on top settle into the underlying wet icing, leaving you with a smooth, flat surface that incorporates the design.

The top details are either piped and left to settle or piped and then "shaped" by dragging a scribe tool or toothpick through them to create desired patterns: from simple polka-dots or lines to more complicated like flowers.
